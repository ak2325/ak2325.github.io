  • Hi! Welcome to my personal website

    I am a radar scientist with an Earth System Science background.

    I am currently a PhD candidate at the University of Texas at Austin. I am expected to graduate by May 2023.

    Facts about My Name

    My birth name is Wu Yue, pronounced as "wú yuè". It consists of two Chinese characters, 吴 (wú) and 越 (yuè).

    Wu is my surname, indicating we are an old and large family originating from 2700 BC. We officially became a family of "Wu" around 12 BC after our ancestors, Tai Bo and Zhong Yong, the first and second sons of the Zhou dynasty, decided to give up their rights to inherit the dynasty and moved to the Lower Reaches of the Yangtze River.

    Since then, our family has survived for thousands of years. Most of us still live near the Yangtze River in the Provinces of Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Anhui, and Shanghai. Nowadays, we are the ninth-largest family in China. In 2018, there were 27.8 million citizens living in Mainland China with a surface of Wu.

    My last name, Yue, is a last name that can be used by all genders and can serve as both an adverb and a verb. As an adverb, it means "better and better"; as a verb, it means "overcoming a barrier, go beyond". It bears my parent's wish that I am always becoming a better person.

    "Sophy" is an English nickname given by my first-grade English teacher, which I like very much and consider part of my name. My family members sometimes call me "Sophy" as well. Therefore, if you have difficulty pronouncing my Chinese name, you are welcome to call me "Sophy".

  • I was born as the only child in my family in Nanjing, Jiangsu, China.

    Jiangsu is located on China's east coast. It is the most densely populated province in China. Although it occupies only 1.12% of the land, it is home to about 6% of China's population.

    When I was in primary school, I wanted to become a doctor so that I could recognize diseases by analyzing their symptoms.

    In junior high, I plan to study archeology in the future because I love solving mysteries and recovering what happened a long time ago. However, I discovered that I am not very good at remembering historical facts and dates.

    In senior high, I realized that I am actually interested in solving a problem by discovering and analyzing details. In the meantime, I started to develop an interest in understanding how the Earth works and in unraveling the mysteries of nature.

    I went to Hong Kong for my undergraduate study majoring in Earth System Science. It was like a dream come true. I was eager to learn all aspects of the Earth System, from the atmosphere to the lithosphere, and explore various research methods, from fieldwork to numerical modeling. I gradually developed an interest in geological and geophysical studies and took courses on earth surface processes, hydrology, and remote sensing.

    I joint Professor Lin Liu's research group in my third year. Under his supervision, I started to learn about and utilize Interferometry Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) for my final year project. I explored the possibility of using InSAR to monitor land subsidence in urban areas, mainly from groundwater extraction.

    I was astonished by the beauty of the fringes in interferograms. I was also surprised how remote sensing can monitor large-scale surface changes without extensive fieldwork. This was when I decided to learn more about InSAR and its applications, and the experience led me to where I am now.

  • Ice Sheet Hydrology

    Around the ice sheet margins melt exceeds local storage capacity and the vast majority of surface meltwater is efficiently routed to the ice-bed interface through moulins. The spring onset of meltwater flux to the bed raises subglacial water pressure at the ice-bed interface because of limited water storage capacity, and decreases friction at the bed, facilitating faster ice flow. Our work focuses on understanding the subglacial hydraulic system including the coupled flow of water and sediments.

    Related Publications:

    Using acoustic sensors to understand buoyant plumes in Alaska (funded by the Institute for Geophysics Blue Sky program)
